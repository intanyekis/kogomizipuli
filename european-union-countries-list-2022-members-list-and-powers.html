As a political and economic union based on the founding treaties, the European Union, with 27 member states, shares the benefits and responsibilities of membership. According to their treaties, some of the duties of their government are shared with the European Union, but not all of them. The state government and union must vote unanimously for some union policies; a qualified majority is required for others.

The EU is unique among other international organizations due to its obligations and shared sovereignty. The ECJ ruled in 1964 that the member states are subject to its legal order, as the founding treaties provide for a legal binding and supreme order. The subsidiarity principle is one of the union’s founding principles, which stipulates that decisions can only be made collectively if they cannot be taken individually.

In addition to the European Commission commissioners, membership in the European Union has other noteworthy features. These groups are comprised of members appointed by member states; however, the members do not represent the interest of their states; instead, they represent the interests of all states participating in the process.
